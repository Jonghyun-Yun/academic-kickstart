Course Content

A comprehensive course including multiple linear regression, non-linear regression and logistic regression. Emphasis is on modeling, inference, diagnostics and application to real data sets. The course begins by developing a toolbox of methods via a sequence of guided homework assignments. It culminates with projects based on consulting-level data analysis problems involving stratification, covariate adjustment and messy data sets.

The course will use R in order to demonstrate the implementation of statistical methods. In the first week, we will have a refresher on the commands in R which you will need to use in the following weeks, but this is not a comprehensive R course, and we will not go in depth on R syntax. Please see online R resources listed in the course website.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of the requirements for this course, students should have the knowledge and skills to:

  1. Calculate/interpret summary and inferential statistics
  2. Infer appropriate conclusions about populations based on data
  3. Combine concepts in new ways to solve a practical problem
  4. Compare and contrast various models
  5. Be proficient with statistical software such as the R language.
  6. Understand how to create reproducible research documents using the R Markdown syntax.

Homework

The homework problems will be assigned weekly on the course webpage. A single homework assignment may be mixed with two types of assignments: 1) Theoretical assignments will be tagged with (Written), and 2) R assignments will require the use of statistical software (R). Statistical software instructions will be provided as needed. Late coursework will not be accepted without a university approved excuse. It is imperative that you show all your work; simply stating an answer will result in no credit for the problem. You are encouraged to work with each other on the homework problems, but you must turn in your own work. There will be 9 homework assignments (30 points each) and a final homework assignment (50 points). Students shall get 100% for homework if they earn no less than 90% of homework. Suppose your total is \(x\) points, then your final score for homework will be calculated as \[ 100 \times \min \left( \frac{x}{0.9 \times 320}, 1 \right) \]

Document Formatting

The R assignments must be turned in electronically, through Blackboard. These assignment will involve writing a combination of code and actual prose. You must submit your assignment in a format which allows for the combination of the two, and the automatic execution of all your code. The easiest way to do this is to use R Markdown. Exceptions may be made, with prior permission, for those who want to use other reproducible dynamic documents. Work should be submitted as R markdown and rendered PDF documents. Every files you submit should have a file name in the following format: WorkType#.FirstName.LastName.

For the theoretical assignments, you have two options: 1) Submitting the assignments to the instructor in class, and 2) Scan them and turn in electronically, through Blackboard. However, it is highly recommended to provide your answers in R markdown format along with the R assignments.

Exam

Final Review Week

A period of five class days prior to the first day of final examinations in the long sessions shall be designated as Final Review Week. The purpose of this week is to allow students sufficient time to prepare for final examinations. During this week, there shall be no scheduled activities such as required field trips or performances; and no instructor shall assign any themes, research problems or exercises of similar scope that have a completion date during or following this week unless specified in the class syllabus. During Final Review Week, an instructor shall not give any examinations constituting 10% or more of the final grade, except makeup tests and laboratory examinations. In addition, no instructor shall give any portion of the final examination during Final Review Week. During this week, classes are held as scheduled. In addition, instructors are not required to limit content to topics that have been previously covered; they may introduce new concepts as appropriate.
